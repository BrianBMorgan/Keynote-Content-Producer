# Anatomy of great keynotes

Load when you want to reference exemplars, teach by example, or pressure-test a draft against the best.

## Steve Jobs — iPhone, January 2007

**What worked:**
- One controlling idea ("three revolutionary products… and they're one device").
- The reveal as the central S.T.A.R. moment.
- Demo-led proof — the product carried the argument.
- Pacing: long setup, slow tension, explosive release.
- Repetition of the "iPod, phone, internet communicator" trio until the audience said it with him.

**Producer's contribution:**
- Months of rehearsal to make the demo look effortless (the famous "golden path" through unstable software).
- Single-screen slides with one assertion at a time.
- Walk-on music, lighting, and rhythm engineered for the reveal.

**Lesson:** Discipline. One idea, one demo, one room.

## Tim Cook — Apple events (ongoing)

**What works:**
- Multi-presenter handoffs choreographed precisely.
- Pre-recorded video segments that allow seamless location/timing flexibility.
- Tight runtime (60–75 min).
- Product-first visuals — the device is the hero.

**Producer's contribution:**
- Pre-record discipline. Cook era treats keynotes as broadcast products, not stage shows.
- Single source of truth for assets across multiple presenters.

**Lesson:** Hybrid recorded + live is a legitimate format. Plan for it.

## Satya Nadella — Microsoft Build / Ignite

**What works:**
- Plain-language framing of complex platform shifts.
- "Tech intensity" and similar repeatable phrases.
- Customer stories interlaced with product demos.
- A genuinely warm executive tone — Microsoft's biggest unlock.

**Producer's contribution:**
- Long-form messaging architecture aligned with Microsoft's category narrative.
- Customer story curation (often year-long sourcing).

**Lesson:** Tone matters. Warmth from a CEO is engineered, not accidental.

## Jensen Huang — Nvidia GTC

**What works:**
- High-information density that respects technical audiences.
- The "stack" framing — full vertical from chips to platforms.
- Long runtime (90–120 min) that works because the audience is paying.
- Showmanship — leather jacket, props, oversized chip reveals.

**Producer's contribution:**
- Permission to be technical because the audience earned it.
- Engineered visual landmarks (the giant GPU on stage).

**Lesson:** Audience determines acceptable density. GTC is not a marketing event.

## Marc Benioff — Dreamforce

**What works:**
- Star power — celebrity speakers, surprise guests, philanthropic causes.
- "Customer 360" and similar repeatable category claims.
- Spectacle scale — tens of thousands in the room.

**Producer's contribution:**
- Talent booking pipeline (often year-round work).
- Massive run-of-show across multiple stages and days.

**Lesson:** Spectacle works when it ladders to message. Star power without message is empty calories.

## Andy Jassy → Matt Garman — AWS re:Invent

**What works:**
- Service-by-service announcement cadence is tedious but trusted — developers come for the news.
- Customer story integration ("here's how Capital One uses…").
- Three-hour runtime offset by clear chapter structure.

**Producer's contribution:**
- Chapter discipline — audience always knows where they are.
- Hundreds of announcement claims legal-cleared in advance.

**Lesson:** For developer audiences, density and trust matter more than spectacle.

## Sundar Pichai — Google I/O

**What works:**
- AI-first framing maintained across all segments.
- Multiple presenters with consistent voice.
- Demo density (live demos still attempted despite risk).

**Producer's contribution:**
- Risk tolerance for live demos with backup paths.
- Visual language unified across product teams.

**Lesson:** Multi-presenter coherence is the hardest thing to do. Treat it as a producer's deliverable, not a default.

## Hans Rosling — TED 2006 "Best stats you've ever seen"

**What works:**
- Data as story, not as evidence.
- Animated visualization carries the argument.
- Speaker physicality — the swordfighter pose with the pointer.

**Producer's contribution:**
- Trust in the speaker. Strip everything else.

**Lesson:** A single great visualization can be the whole keynote.

## Al Gore — An Inconvenient Truth, TED 2006

**What works:**
- The hockey-stick visual as S.T.A.R. moment.
- "What is" / "what could be" alternation at scale.
- Emotional landing followed by call to action.

**Producer's contribution:**
- Visual engineering — the chart was redesigned dozens of times.

**Lesson:** A great visual is engineered, not found.

## OpenAI — multimodal product demos (2024 GPT-4o, 2025 follow-ons)

**What works:**
- Live interaction with the model as the entire argument.
- Honest acknowledgment of limitations on stage.
- Small stage, small team, big reveal.

**Producer's contribution:**
- Demo failure-proofing under camera.
- Brevity — under 30 minutes, dense.

**Lesson:** When the product can speak for itself, get out of its way.

## Common patterns across all of the above

- One controlling idea, repeated.
- One engineered S.T.A.R. moment.
- Customer or audience stake, not corporate self-celebration.
- Visual restraint — one claim per moment.
- Pacing variety — peaks and valleys.
- Conviction from the speaker, earned in rehearsal.
- Production invisibility — the audience sees the show, not the seams.
