# Narrative frameworks — when to use which

Load this when the user asks for story structure, arc design, or message architecture. Pick one frame per keynote — do not mix.

## The frames

### Duarte sparkline (default for tech keynotes)
Alternate "what is" vs "what could be." Builds tension and release. End on a "new bliss" — the future state the audience now believes is possible.

**Use when:** product launch, category shift, vision keynote.
**Avoid when:** straightforward operational update or earnings-style readout.

### Problem / solution
Name the audience's pain in their words. Present the solution. Prove it. Close with what they should do.

**Use when:** B2B keynote with clear ICP pain, sales-adjacent shows.
**Avoid when:** the "problem" is contrived or insults the audience's intelligence.

### Before / after / bridge
Show today's reality, paint tomorrow's reality, then the bridge from one to the other (your product, your category, your call to action).

**Use when:** transformation stories, internal all-hands, change management.
**Avoid when:** the "after" is unprovable in the room.

### What / so what / now what
Information → implication → action. Crisp, executive-friendly.

**Use when:** investor updates, board briefings, analyst day segments.
**Avoid when:** you need emotional movement; this frame is rational.

### Hero's journey
Audience is the hero, you are the guide. Call to adventure, refusal, mentor, threshold, ordeal, return with the elixir.

**Use when:** developer keynotes, community shows, customer success stories.
**Avoid when:** time is tight; the frame needs room to breathe.

### Customer transformation
Open with a customer's "before." Walk through their journey. End with their "after." Your product is the supporting character.

**Use when:** customer conferences, sales kickoffs, case-study segments.
**Avoid when:** the customer hasn't given air-tight approval to the story.

### Category shift
Name the old game. Declare the new game. Position the audience on the right side of the shift.

**Use when:** category-creation moments, repositioning keynotes (e.g., "the era of X is over").
**Avoid when:** you can't actually defend the category claim — analysts will eat you alive.

### Demo-led proof
The demo IS the argument. Setup → reveal → implication. Minimal slide scaffolding.

**Use when:** product launches where the product speaks for itself (Jobs/iPhone 2007, OpenAI demos).
**Avoid when:** demo confidence is low or the product isn't yet visually obvious.

## S.T.A.R. moments (Duarte)

"Something They'll Always Remember." Plan at least one per major keynote. Categories:

- **The reveal** — Jobs pulling iPhone from his pocket; Apple pulling MacBook Air from a manila envelope.
- **The repeatable phrase** — Nadella's "tech intensity," Benioff's "Customer 360."
- **The visual** — Al Gore's hockey stick, Huang's giant GPU on stage.
- **The stunt** — Gates releasing mosquitoes at TED, OpenAI live multimodal demos.
- **The guest** — surprise customer, surprise celebrity, surprise founder return.
- **The number** — one stunning statistic the audience repeats afterward.

A S.T.A.R. moment is engineered, not accidental. If you don't have one by Phase 2, invent one or accept the keynote will be forgettable.

## Slide / visual frameworks

### Assertion-evidence
Every slide title is a claim (a complete sentence), not a topic label. Body is visual evidence for the claim.

- Wrong: "Q4 Performance"
- Right: "Q4 revenue grew 38%, led by enterprise contracts over $1M"

### Presentation Zen (Garr Reynolds)
- Slides support the speaker; they are not the speaker.
- Use empty space. Avoid clutter.
- Visual > textual. One idea per slide.

### Storytelling with Data (Cole Nussbaumer Knaflic)
- Don't show data — tell a story with it.
- Eliminate clutter. Use color to focus, not decorate.
- Always name the "so what" beside the chart.

### 10/20/30 (Guy Kawasaki)
10 slides, 20 minutes, 30-point font minimum. Heuristic for pitch decks; useful pressure-test for any executive talk.

## Storytelling primitives worth borrowing

### Pixar's 22 (operating prompts for keynote writers)
- "You admire a character for trying more than for their successes."
- "Once upon a time… every day… one day… because of that… because of that… until finally…"
- "Why must you tell THIS story? What's the belief burning within you?"
- "Discount the 1st thing that comes to mind, and the 2nd, 3rd, 4th, 5th — get the obvious out of the way."

### Aristotle's three-act structure
Setup → confrontation → resolution. The oldest reliable shape; works for any 30-60 minute keynote.

### Tension and release
Audiences need both. A keynote that is all peaks is exhausting; all valleys is boring. Plan the emotional contour explicitly: where are the laughs, the gasps, the proofs, the pauses.

## How to pick

Ask, in order:

1. **What does the audience need to believe afterward?** → that names the destination.
2. **What do they believe now?** → that names the starting tension.
3. **Is there a customer story strong enough to carry the arc?** → if yes, customer transformation.
4. **Is the product the proof?** → if yes, demo-led.
5. **Is this a category bet?** → if yes, category shift or sparkline.
6. **Is the audience rational (investors/analysts)?** → what/so what/now what.
7. **Otherwise:** default to Duarte sparkline.
