# Speaker development

Load when prepping an executive for delivery, formatting a teleprompter script, or designing a rehearsal cadence.

## Write for the ear, not the eye

The single most common failure: scripts written like a press release. Symptoms include long sentences, subordinate clauses, formal vocabulary, and no contractions. The fix:

- **Sentences ≤ 15–20 words.** Break long thoughts into beats.
- **Contractions on.** "We're," "you're," "it's." Executives without contractions sound robotic.
- **One idea per sentence.** Compound sentences kill teleprompter pacing.
- **Read aloud test.** If you can't say it in one breath, rewrite.
- **No nested clauses.** Subordinate clauses derail the prompter.
- **Concrete > abstract.** "Our 10,000 enterprise customers" beats "our broad customer base."
- **Numbers are landmarks.** "$1.2 billion" sticks; "significant growth" doesn't.
- **Pronouns work.** "You" pulls the audience in; "we" includes them; "they" distances.

## Teleprompter formatting conventions

- **Font size:** Start at 48–60pt for typical on-camera setups with the speaker 5–8 feet from the prompter.
- **Line length:** Short. ~6–8 words per line is comfortable.
- **All caps:** Avoid — they slow reading and feel like shouting.
- **Punctuation:** Use commas, em-dashes, and ellipses to drive cadence. Periods = full stops, hard breaths.
- **Stage directions:** Brackets, italicized, separate line. `[PAUSE]`, `[BEAT]`, `[GESTURE TO SCREEN]`, `[VIDEO ROLLS]`.
- **Cue callouts:** ALL CAPS in brackets on their own line. `[CUE: VIDEO 003 — "Customer Story: Acme"]`
- **Pronunciation guides:** Inline phonetic for any name the speaker might trip on. `Saoirse (SUR-sha)`.
- **Builds:** Mark slide builds in the margin so the speaker knows what's appearing as they speak.
- **Speaker handoffs:** Bold, distinct formatting. Walk-on/walk-off music cues noted.

## Rehearsal cadence

Move from low fidelity to high fidelity. Each rehearsal has a purpose; do not collapse them.

| Rehearsal | Purpose | Fidelity |
|---|---|---|
| Table read | Hear the script aloud, time it, find clunky language | Seated, no slides |
| Standing read | Body in motion, blocking notes | Standing, paper script |
| Click-through | Confirm script + slides + transitions | Speaker + deck, no AV |
| Demo rehearsal | Confirm demo flow, reset, backup | Demo machine + operator |
| Tech rehearsal | Confirm all cues, AV, playback, lighting | Full crew, partial speakers ok |
| Cue-to-cue | Operators only — every cue called, no full performance | Crew, no audience |
| Dress rehearsal | Full run as if live | Full crew, full speakers, full audience simulation |
| Final polish | Speaker confidence, last cuts, last nerves | 1:1 with each executive |

**Minimum acceptable:** Table read + click-through + tech rehearsal + dress. Anything less is malpractice for a flagship keynote.

## Rehearsal note categories

Always capture in these buckets — never freeform:

- **Story clarity** — was the beat understood?
- **Cuts for time** — what to remove if we're long
- **Pronunciation** — anything the speaker tripped on
- **Slide timing** — builds in/out of sync
- **Demo confidence** — would I bet the show on this demo as currently configured?
- **Teleprompter friction** — line breaks, font size, scroll speed
- **Body/blocking** — where the speaker stands, when they move, where they look
- **Confidence-monitor needs** — what should appear on the floor monitor
- **Approvals** — what is still unapproved by legal / PR / IR / brand

## Confidence-monitor patterns

The confidence monitor (downstage, facing the speaker) shows:

- The slide currently on the main screens (so the speaker knows what the audience sees)
- A countdown timer for the segment
- Sometimes: the next slide or the next bullet
- Sometimes: a producer note ("SLOW DOWN," "WRAP," "GO TO DEMO")

Decide upfront what the speaker sees. Changing it on show day rattles them.

## Q&A and bridge prep

For any keynote with press, analyst, or investor exposure:

- Draft 10 likely questions, ranked by sensitivity.
- For each, build a 30-second bridge: acknowledge → reframe → key message.
- Identify any topic the speaker should decline ("I can't comment on that today").
- Pre-clear sensitive language with legal/IR.

## Executive coaching prompts

When the executive is good but not great, push on these:

- **Pacing:** Where are you rushing? Where are you holding?
- **Eye lines:** When do you look at camera vs. the room?
- **Hands:** Comfortable, intentional gestures, or are they distracting?
- **Vocal variety:** Are you using your full range, or stuck in one register?
- **The pause:** Are you using silence as a tool, or filling it with "um"?
- **Conviction:** Do you believe what you're saying? The audience can tell.

## Things to never do

- Surprise the executive with new content on show day.
- Let a speaker rehearse without the actual deck.
- Approve a script the speaker hasn't read aloud.
- Skip the demo rehearsal because "it always works."
- Change confidence-monitor content the day of.
- Let the executive write their own teleprompter cues.
